A 1990s Theorem Just Became the Default Network Across Most New AWS Data Centers

By Pratik Dhanave
AWSNetworkingGraph TheoryData CenterSpraypoint

AWS disclosed that Resilient Network Graphs (RNG) — a flat network design based on quasi-random graph theory — is now the default for most new non-GPU data center builds globally. The core idea was proven by mathematicians ~30 years ago: randomly connecting routers produces a remarkably efficient and resilient topology. It took three decades to turn that into production hardware.

What stood out to me:

  • The numbers are hard to ignore — 69% fewer networking devices, up to 33% higher throughput, and a projected 40% reduction in network power consumption versus traditional fat-tree designs.
  • The hierarchy is gone — Fat-tree networks route traffic up through ToR → aggregation → spine switches and back down. RNG replaces that with a mesh where every rack connects directly to a quasi-random set of other racks. No tiers to climb, no shared spine to congest.
  • ShuffleBox solves the cabling problem — You can't run random wires across a hyperscale facility. AWS built a passive optical device with internally shuffled fiber, so the logical topology is random while physical cabling stays simple. Being passive, it adds no latency, no power draw, and no new failure mode.
  • Spraypoint handles routing — With no hierarchy to guide packets, AWS sprays traffic across neighboring routers and uses waypoints to steer it home. Counterintuitive, but it turns built-in redundancy into usable bandwidth — reportedly nearly twice the independent paths of shortest-path routing.
  • Failures degrade gracefully — Lose 1% of routers, lose ~1% of capacity. In a fat-tree, losing one spine switch can cascade into bottlenecks for every rack beneath it. This was the property I found most compelling.

One important caveat: RNG targets general-purpose compute where traffic looks random. AI training generates coordinated, centralized traffic, so AWS still runs its UltraServer architecture for GPU clusters. (RNG has already rolled out in data centers in Spain and Germany, with broader global deployment underway.)

A nice reminder that foundational research can sit on the shelf for decades before the engineering catches up to make it practical at scale. Do you see expander-based fabrics becoming an industry pattern, or staying an AWS-specific advantage?
